## Culture by Design, Not by Default

- Culture happens whether you design it or not -- the question is whether it serves your organization
- Most organizations have accidental cultures shaped by early hires, crises, and unchallenged assumptions
- HR leaders must intentionally build, maintain, and sometimes repair organizational culture
- Culture isn't what you write on the wall -- it's what happens when the boss isn't watching

> Culture is what people do when no one is looking. If you want to know your real culture, watch what gets tolerated.

<!-- notes: Ask: "What's the gap between your stated culture and your actual culture?" Every organization has one. The HR leader's role is to close that gap -- not by writing better value statements, but by designing systems that reinforce desired behaviors. -->

## The Culture Stack

- **Layer 1: Psychological Safety** (from Module 3)
  - The foundation -- without safety, no authentic culture can take root
  - People must feel safe to speak up, take risks, and be themselves
- **Layer 2: Behavioral Norms**
  - Specific, observable behaviors that define "how we work here"
  - Not vague values like "integrity" -- concrete behaviors like "we address disagreements directly within 48 hours"
- **Layer 3: Systems & Structures**
  - Hiring criteria, promotion criteria, performance evaluation, recognition systems
  - Culture is reinforced or undermined by every organizational system
- **Layer 4: Leadership Modeling**
  - Leaders' behavior trumps every other cultural signal
  - What leaders pay attention to, reward, and tolerate defines culture

<!-- notes: Walk through each layer with examples. The key insight: most culture efforts fail because they focus on Layer 2 (behavioral norms) without addressing Layers 1, 3, and 4. You can't declare a culture into existence -- you have to build it systemically. -->

## From Values to Behaviors

- Values are meaningless without behavioral anchors
- **The translation exercise:**
  - Value: "Innovation" -- Behavior: "Every team runs at least one experiment per quarter and shares learnings publicly"
  - Value: "Collaboration" -- Behavior: "Before launching any cross-functional initiative, we identify and consult all affected teams"
  - Value: "Accountability" -- Behavior: "When something goes wrong, the first question is 'What can we learn?' not 'Who's to blame?'"
- Test your behavioral anchors: Could a new employee observe this behavior on their first day? If not, it's not concrete enough

<!-- notes: Have participants take one of their organization's stated values and translate it into 2-3 specific, observable behaviors. This exercise reveals how vague most value statements are -- and how much work is needed to make them actionable. -->

## Measuring Culture

- Move beyond engagement surveys to track culture health:
  - **Behavioral indicators:** Are people actually doing what the culture promises? (Observable, countable)
  - **Decision pattern analysis:** How do decisions get made in practice? (Follow the actual process, not the org chart)
  - **Leading indicators:** Signals that predict cultural drift before it becomes crisis
    - Voluntary turnover in key populations
    - Internal mobility rates
    - Time to fill positions (employer brand signal)
    - Employee referral rates
    - Psychological safety pulse surveys
- The discipline: measure culture as rigorously as you measure financial performance

> If you can't measure it, you can't manage it. And if you only measure engagement annually, you're looking at a rear-view mirror.
