# The Dual Ladder
## Supporting ICs & Managing Managers

## The Dual Career Ladder

- Some of your best people want to stay technical (IC track); others want to lead people (management track)
- Supporting both is essential -- forcing everyone into management loses your best engineers
- **Common failures:**
  - Making management the only path to senior compensation
  - Treating IC leaders as "just" engineers with a fancy title
  - Not giving IC leaders real organizational influence
  - Promoting great engineers into management they don't want
- **A healthy dual ladder:**
  - Equivalent compensation at equivalent levels
  - Equivalent voice in strategy and decision-making
  - Clear expectations and progression for both tracks
  - Ability to move between tracks without stigma

> The best engineering organizations don't force a choice between building things and leading people. They value both -- and mean it.

<!-- notes: Ask: "In your organization, is there truly equivalent respect and compensation for senior ICs and senior managers?" Most will say no. This gap is the #1 reason senior engineers either leave or reluctantly take management roles they don't want. -->

## Building the IC Track

- **What senior ICs need:**
  - Technical scope and challenge that matches their level
  - Organizational influence -- inclusion in architecture decisions, strategy conversations, hiring
  - Mentoring opportunities -- both giving and receiving
  - Visibility -- their work should be seen and valued by leadership
  - Growth path -- clear progression from Senior to Staff to Principal to Distinguished
- **What senior ICs don't need:**
  - Being treated as individual heroes instead of force multipliers
  - Isolation from organizational decisions
  - Being "managed" by someone less technically capable without understanding of their work
- IC leadership means multiplying impact through technical influence, not through direct reports

<!-- notes: The IC track is often poorly defined compared to the management track. Have participants sketch their organization's IC progression -- titles, expectations, scope of influence at each level. If it's vague, that's the problem to solve. -->

## Managing Managers -- A Fundamentally Different Job

- Managing managers is NOT the same as managing ICs -- the skills that made you a great first-line manager may not transfer
- **Key differences:**
  - **Your leverage is indirect:** You influence outcomes through people who influence outcomes
  - **Your information is filtered:** You hear what your managers choose to share
  - **Your role is systems, not tasks:** You design the environment; they operate within it
  - **Your coaching shifts:** From "how to do the work" to "how to lead the people doing the work"
- Connecting to Plugin D (Leadership Pipeline) and Module 3's coaching framework:
  - Coach your managers on their coaching
  - Build their capability to build capability

> As a manager of managers, your success is two layers removed from the work. If that makes you uncomfortable, you haven't fully transitioned.

## The Skip-Level Relationship

- **Why skip-levels matter:**
  - Direct connection to the front line -- unfiltered signal
  - Demonstrates that leadership cares about individual contributors
  - Catches problems that your managers might miss or minimize
  - Builds organizational trust beyond the immediate reporting line
- **How to do skip-levels well:**
  - Regular cadence (monthly or quarterly) -- not just when there's a problem
  - Clarify the purpose: "I'm here to listen and understand, not to override your manager"
  - Ask about the environment, not the manager: "What's working well? What could be better?"
  - Follow up through your manager, not around them
  - Never use skip-level information to undermine your managers

<!-- notes: Skip-levels are powerful but can be misused. If ICs learn they can go around their manager to you, it undermines the management chain. If managers learn you're gathering information behind their backs, it erodes trust. The key: transparency about purpose and consistent follow-through. -->

## Retention & Growth Across Both Tracks

- **Top ICs leave when:**
  - The only path to recognition or compensation is management
  - Their work is invisible to leadership
  - They have no organizational influence
  - They feel stagnant technically
- **Top managers burn out when:**
  - They have no peer support network
  - They're promoted without training or coaching
  - They're held accountable for outcomes without authority to drive them
  - The organization doesn't invest in management development
- **Designing for retention:**
  - Equitable compensation and visibility across both tracks
  - Peer support groups for managers (manager circles, coaching cohorts)
  - Technical forums and communities for senior ICs
  - Regular career conversations that explore both tracks openly

> You don't retain great people with titles and compensation alone. You retain them with growth, impact, and the feeling that their work matters.
