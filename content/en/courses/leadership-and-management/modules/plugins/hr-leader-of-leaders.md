## Coaching & Influencing Without Formal Authority

## The HR Influence Paradox

- HR leaders are expected to shape leadership across the entire organization
- Yet they rarely have positional authority over the leaders they influence
- This is the influence-without-authority challenge at its most extreme
- The paradox: the more you try to enforce good leadership, the more resistance you create
- The solution: earn influence through credibility, not compliance

> HR's greatest power isn't the policy manual -- it's the ability to make leaders better at leading.

<!-- notes: Ask HR participants: "How often do you feel caught between knowing what should happen and having the authority to make it happen?" This tension defines the HR leadership role. Validating it opens the conversation. -->

## The Research: What Makes HR Influential?

- **Dave Ulrich's HR Competency Model** (based on 30 years of research with 80,000+ respondents) identifies the key competencies that distinguish effective HR leaders:
  1. **Strategic Positioner:** Understands the business context and translates it into HR priorities
  2. **Credible Activist:** Builds personal trust while driving results -- "credible" means trusted, "activist" means proactive
  3. **Paradox Navigator:** Manages inherent tensions (employee vs. business, short-term vs. long-term)
  4. **Culture and Change Champion:** Shapes organizational culture and leads transformation
- **Key finding:** The #1 predictor of HR effectiveness is **personal credibility** -- not HR technical knowledge, not policy expertise, not process efficiency
- **The implication:** Your influence as an HR leader is built on who you are and how you show up, not on your title or your policy manual

> HR influence is earned in every interaction. It cannot be mandated by the org chart.

<!-- notes: Ulrich's research is foundational for HR professionals. The insight that personal credibility outranks technical competence is often surprising and liberating -- it means the path to influence is through relationship and business acumen, not through deeper HR technical knowledge. -->

## The Credibility Equation for HR

- Shift your language from compliance to business impact:
  - Instead of "HR says we have to..." try "Here's the business risk if we don't..."
  - Instead of "The policy requires..." try "Here's what happens to retention when..."
  - Instead of "You need to do a performance review" try "Here's how this conversation will help you hit your Q3 targets"
- **Build trust through:**
  - Speaking in business outcomes, not HR processes
  - Understanding the business as deeply as you understand people
  - Delivering insights before being asked
  - Following through consistently -- reliability is your currency

<!-- notes: The credibility shift is the single most important takeaway for HR leaders. When HR speaks the language of business risk, cost, and strategic impact, they get invited into rooms they used to be excluded from. -->

## Exercise: The Language Shift

- **Step 1 (3 min, individual):** Write down three common statements you make to business leaders in a typical week. Write them exactly as you normally say them.
- **Step 2 (4 min, individual):** Rewrite each statement using the language shift:
  - Replace "HR requires" with "The business risk is"
  - Replace "Policy says" with "Data shows"
  - Replace "You should" with "Managers who do X see Y result"
  - Replace "Compliance" with "Risk mitigation" or "Competitive advantage"
- **Step 3 (3 min, pairs):** Read both versions to a partner. Discuss:
  - Which version is more likely to get a positive response?
  - Does the rewrite preserve the intent of the original message?
  - Where is the line between translation and manipulation?

> The goal isn't to hide HR's role -- it's to frame HR's guidance in terms the business values and responds to.

<!-- notes: The "manipulation" question is important to address directly. Translation is not manipulation -- it's meeting your audience where they are. The underlying message doesn't change; the packaging does. If the substance changes, you've crossed the line. Allow 10 minutes for this exercise. -->

## The Four Hats of the HR Leader

- HR leaders wear multiple hats depending on the situation -- knowing which hat to wear is a critical skill
- **The Coach:** Asking questions, helping leaders discover their own solutions. Use when the leader has capability but needs perspective.
- **The Advisor:** Offering expert guidance based on experience and data. Use when the leader faces a novel challenge and needs expertise.
- **The Challenger:** Pushing back on decisions that carry risk. Use when the leader is about to make a mistake with significant people or legal consequences.
- **The Partner:** Co-creating solutions alongside business leaders. Use when the challenge requires both business and people expertise to solve.
- **The common mistake:** Wearing the same hat in every situation -- usually Coach or Challenger, depending on personality
- **The skill:** Reading the situation and choosing the right hat. Sometimes leaders need a coach; sometimes they need someone to say "Stop."

<!-- notes: Have participants reflect on which hat they wear most often (their default) and which they wear least often (their growth edge). Most HR leaders default to either Coach or Challenger. The Partner hat is often the most underdeveloped but the most valuable for building long-term influence. -->

## Coaching Without Authority

- **The coaching stance for HR:**
  - Ask questions before giving advice -- "What have you considered?"
  - Share observations, not directives -- "I've noticed..." not "You should..."
  - Offer frameworks, not prescriptions -- give tools and let them choose
  - Be a mirror, not a judge -- reflect what you see and let them draw conclusions
- **Building trust with skeptical managers:**
  - Start with their agenda, not yours
  - Demonstrate understanding of their pressures before offering solutions
  - Show quick wins before tackling systemic issues
  - Be consistent: confidentiality and follow-through build trust over time

> The best HR leaders don't tell managers what to do -- they help managers see what they couldn't see on their own.

## Role-Play: The Reluctant Manager

- **Setup:** An HR Business Partner needs to coach a senior operations manager whose team has the highest turnover in the company. The manager believes the turnover is "just the nature of the work" and doesn't see it as a leadership issue.
- **Round 1 (3 min):** Partner A (HR) uses the traditional approach -- presents the turnover data, explains the cost, and recommends specific changes
- **Round 2 (3 min):** Partner A (HR) uses the coaching approach:
  - Start with curiosity: "Help me understand what your team's experience is like day to day"
  - Share data as a question: "What do you make of the fact that your team's turnover is 2x the company average?"
  - Connect to their goals: "How is the turnover affecting your ability to hit your operational targets?"
  - Offer partnership: "What if we looked at this together and identified two things we could try?"
- **Debrief questions:**
  - Which approach created more openness?
  - Which approach is more likely to lead to sustained change?
  - When might the direct approach actually be necessary?

<!-- notes: This role-play is the experiential core of the module. The contrast between telling and coaching is dramatic in practice. Allow 10 minutes total. -->

## Navigating Political Dynamics

- HR leaders operate in complex political environments where competing interests are the norm
- **Common political challenges:**
  - **The executive who resists HR input:** Build influence gradually -- start with their pain points, not your agenda
  - **The conflict between two senior leaders:** Stay neutral, facilitate dialogue, resist being pulled to a side
  - **The culture of "going around HR":** Don't fight it with policy enforcement -- make yourself so valuable that people come to you voluntarily
  - **The "shoot the messenger" dynamic:** When you surface uncomfortable truths, some leaders will blame HR for the problem. Build alliances so you're not isolated.
- **Allan Cohen and David Bradford's "Influence Without Authority" model:**
  - Identify what you want (your goal)
  - Understand what the other person values (their "currencies")
  - Make exchanges: offer what they value in return for what you need
  - Build relationships before you need them

> Political savvy isn't about playing games -- it's about understanding how organizations actually work and navigating that reality with integrity.

## Building a Network of Influence

- Your influence as an HR leader extends through relationships, not reporting lines
- **The Strategic Relationship Map:**
  - **Champions:** Leaders who actively support HR's strategic role. Nurture these relationships -- they're your credibility amplifiers.
  - **Allies:** Leaders who are open to partnership but haven't fully engaged. Invest time here -- these are your growth opportunities.
  - **Skeptics:** Leaders who view HR as administrative overhead. Don't write them off -- find their pain points and demonstrate value.
  - **Resistors:** Leaders who actively resist HR involvement. Manage these relationships carefully -- sometimes through their champions.
- **The 3-1 Rule:** For every ask you make of a business leader, ensure you've delivered at least three value-adds first. Deposits before withdrawals.
- **Quarterly relationship audit:** Review your Strategic Relationship Map. Are you investing time in the right places?

<!-- notes: The Strategic Relationship Map makes relationship management concrete and strategic. Have participants sketch their map quickly -- champions, allies, skeptics, resistors. Most HR leaders under-invest in the ally and skeptic categories. -->

## Data as Credibility: The HR Analytics Edge

- In organizations that value data, HR leaders gain credibility by bringing evidence, not just intuition
- **High-impact analytics for HR credibility:**
  - **Turnover cost analysis:** "Each departure costs 1.5-2x annual salary. Our 18% turnover rate represents $X million in annual cost."
  - **Engagement-performance correlation:** "Teams in the top quartile of engagement outperform bottom-quartile teams by 21% in profitability" (Gallup, 2023)
  - **Manager effectiveness impact:** "Managers account for 70% of variance in team engagement scores" (Gallup)
  - **Time-to-productivity:** "Our onboarding improvements reduced time-to-productivity from 6 months to 4 months -- a $X saving per hire"
- **The data storytelling principle from F1 applies here:** Don't just present the data -- tell the story. Context, Insight, Implication, Action.
- **Caution:** Data supports credibility but doesn't replace relationship. Analytics without partnership becomes surveillance.

> Data gives HR a seat at the table. Relationships keep you there.

<!-- notes: The analytics dimension is increasingly important for HR credibility. However, emphasize the caution: HR data about people can feel invasive if not handled with care. ## Earning a Seat at the Table

- The "seat at the table" isn't given -- it's earned through repeated impact
- **How HR leaders build strategic relevance:**
  - Bring data that connects people trends to business outcomes
  - Anticipate leadership challenges before they become crises
  - Be the person leaders call when they're struggling, not just when they have to
  - Build relationships in calm times so you're trusted in crisis
  - Own your expertise with confidence -- you ARE the people expert

<!-- notes: This slide builds on everything covered so far. The "seat at the table" is the cumulative result of credibility, coaching skill, political navigation, data fluency, and consistent value delivery. It's not a one-time achievement but an ongoing practice. -->

## The Difficult Moments: When Influence Isn't Enough

- Sometimes coaching, partnership, and influence aren't sufficient -- and HR must escalate
- **When to shift from influence to escalation:**
  - Legal or ethical violations that create organizational liability
  - Patterns of behavior that harm employees and resist coaching intervention
  - Decisions that create significant retention or reputational risk
  - Safety concerns that require immediate action
- **How to escalate without destroying relationships:**
  - Be transparent: "I've tried to work with you on this. I now need to raise it because the organizational risk is too significant."
  - Separate the person from the problem: "I respect you and I believe in your leadership. AND this specific situation requires intervention."
  - Document your coaching efforts: show that escalation was a last resort, not a first response
  - Follow through: if you escalate, see it through. Starting and stopping destroys credibility.

> Knowing when to shift from coaching to escalation -- and doing so with integrity -- is the mark of a mature HR leader.

## Key Takeaways

- **Personal credibility** is the #1 predictor of HR effectiveness -- not technical knowledge or policy expertise
- **Speak business language:** Replace compliance framing with risk, cost, and strategic impact framing
- **Wear the right hat:** Coach, Advisor, Challenger, or Partner -- read the situation and choose
- Use the **coaching stance** (questions before advice, observations before directives) as your default
- Build a **Strategic Relationship Map** and invest in allies and skeptics, not just champions
- **Data builds credibility** -- but only when combined with strong relationships and business understanding
- The **seat at the table** is earned one credible conversation at a time

> HR's influence isn't about authority. It's about being the leader that other leaders turn to when the stakes are high and the answers aren't clear.
