# The IC-to-Manager Transition
## Navigating the Identity Shift from Builder to Leader

<!-- notes: Addresses the identity shift from individual contributor to manager. What made you successful as an engineer can actively undermine you as a manager. -->

---

## The Identity Shift

- What made you successful as an engineer -- deep focus, technical mastery, solving problems yourself -- can actively undermine you as a manager
- The transition isn't a promotion -- it's a career change
- Your value is no longer in what YOU produce -- it's in what your TEAM produces
- The hardest part: letting go of the identity that earned you respect
- Many new managers never fully make this transition -- they become "senior ICs with reports"

> The best engineers solve problems. The best engineering managers create environments where problems get solved -- by others.

<!-- notes: Open with a question: "How many of you still write code regularly?" For new managers, this is often a coping mechanism -- returning to what feels productive. Validate the discomfort, then challenge the assumption that personal technical output is their primary value. -->

## The Research: Why This Transition Fails

- A 2023 study by the Engineering Leadership Institute found that **60% of new engineering managers** consider returning to IC roles within the first 18 months
- **Linda Hill's research at Harvard Business School** ("Becoming a Manager") identifies that the transition from IC to manager is fundamentally about identity transformation, not skill acquisition
- The **Peter Principle** (Laurence J. Peter, 1969) explains why: organizations promote based on current performance, not readiness for the next role
- **Key finding:** The skills that correlate with IC promotion (technical depth, individual speed, autonomous problem-solving) have a **negative correlation** with management effectiveness
- Research from Google's **Project Oxygen** identified that the #1 behavior of great managers is "being a good coach" -- not technical expertise

> The moment you become a manager, you are a beginner again. The sooner you accept that, the faster you grow.

<!-- notes: The research gives participants permission to struggle. It's not their personal failing -- it's a well-documented, structural challenge. The Google Project Oxygen data is particularly powerful for engineering audiences because it comes from an engineering-first organization. -->

## New Engineering Manager Traps

- **The hero coding trap:** Jumping in to write code when the team is stuck -- feels productive but undermines team growth
- **The "just let me do it" instinct:** Doing work yourself because it's faster than teaching someone else
- **Measuring productivity by code output:** Your PRs are down, but are your team's PRs up?
- **Solving instead of coaching:** Giving technical solutions instead of developing problem-solving capability
- **Avoiding people problems:** Retreating to technical work because people issues are uncomfortable
- Building on Module 1's self-awareness: what's your default under pressure -- doing or leading?

> If you're the smartest engineer in the room, you might be a great IC. If you make everyone else smarter, you're a great manager.

<!-- notes: Have participants identify which trap they fall into most. Be specific: "In the last month, when did you do work that someone on your team could have done?" The pattern reveals where they need to let go. -->

## Exercise: The Trap Audit

- **Step 1 (3 min, individual):** Review your calendar and task list from the past two weeks
- **Step 2:** Mark each activity as one of:
  - **M** = Management work (1:1s, coaching, planning, unblocking, hiring)
  - **IC** = Individual contributor work (coding, debugging, writing specs you could have delegated)
  - **H** = Hybrid (architecture review, code review for learning, technical mentoring)
- **Step 3:** Calculate your M / IC / H ratio
- **Step 4 (5 min, pairs):** Share your ratio with a partner and discuss:
  - What IC work could you have delegated?
  - What management work are you avoiding?
  - What would your ideal ratio look like in 6 months?

> Target ratio for new managers: at least 60% M, no more than 20% IC, 20% H. If you're below 40% M, you haven't made the transition yet.

<!-- notes: This exercise creates concrete self-awareness. Most new managers are shocked by how much IC work they're still doing. The partner discussion adds accountability and often surfaces blind spots. Allow 10 minutes total. -->

## Redefining Productivity

- **Old productivity metrics (IC):** Lines of code, PRs merged, bugs fixed, features shipped personally
- **New productivity metrics (Manager):**
  - Team velocity and throughput
  - Capability growth across the team
  - Unblocking speed -- how fast do you remove obstacles?
  - Retention -- are your best people staying and growing?
  - Code review quality -- is the team getting better through feedback?
  - Decision-making speed -- are decisions flowing or stuck?
- **The manager's daily question:** "What did I do today that made my team more effective?"
- Your output is now multiplied through others -- a 10% improvement across 8 people exceeds any solo contribution

<!-- notes: Have participants write their "manager productivity dashboard" -- 3-5 metrics they'll track for themselves. The exercise forces them to articulate what "good" looks like in their new role, which many haven't done explicitly. -->

## The Multiplier Effect

- **Andy Grove's "High Output Management"** defines a manager's output as the output of their organization plus the output of neighboring organizations under their influence
- Consider the math:
  - You as an IC: 1x output (your work)
  - You managing 6 engineers: potential 6x-10x output (their work, amplified by your leadership)
  - Every hour you spend coding: +1 unit of output for you, 0 for the team
  - Every hour you spend coaching: +0 for you directly, but potentially +0.5 units per person = +3 units total
- **The leverage question:** Is this the highest-leverage use of my time right now?
- **Three high-leverage activities for engineering managers:**
  1. Giving timely, specific feedback that changes behavior
  2. Making or improving a process that affects the whole team
  3. Hiring or developing someone who will produce output for years

> Think in terms of leverage, not effort. A 30-minute 1:1 that prevents a key engineer from quitting is worth more than a week of your coding.

<!-- notes: Andy Grove's framework resonates with engineers because it's mathematical and logical. The leverage concept gives them a new mental model for evaluating how they spend their time. Ask: "What was your highest-leverage activity this week?" -->

## The First 90 Days: A Roadmap

- **Days 1-30: Listen and Learn**
  - Conduct listening tours with every team member (30-min 1:1s)
  - Ask: "What's working? What's broken? What would you change if you were in my role?"
  - Map the team's skills, motivations, and growth areas
  - Understand the existing processes before changing anything
- **Days 31-60: Establish Rhythms**
  - Set up weekly 1:1s with every direct report (30 min minimum)
  - Establish team rituals: standups, retrospectives, planning cadence
  - Identify and execute one quick win that demonstrates your value as a manager
  - Start building relationships with peer managers and your own manager
- **Days 61-90: Shape Direction**
  - Articulate a team vision and share it for feedback
  - Begin addressing the systemic issues you identified in month 1
  - Have explicit career development conversations with each report
  - Set 90-day goals with your manager for your own managerial development

<!-- notes: The roadmap gives structure to a period that often feels chaotic. Emphasize: the biggest first-90-days mistake is trying to change things too fast. Your first job is to earn trust through listening and reliability, not through grand plans. -->

## Mastering the 1:1

- The 1:1 is the most important tool in a manager's toolkit -- it is your primary work now
- **The structure (Michael Lopp, "Managing Humans"):**
  - **Their agenda first:** "What's on your mind?" -- let them set the topics
  - **Status updates last:** Don't waste 1:1 time on information that could be async
  - **Career and growth regularly:** At least once per month, dedicate the 1:1 to development
- **Questions that unlock real conversation:**
  - "What's the hardest part of your week right now?"
  - "What's something you wish I would stop doing?"
  - "Where do you feel stuck, and what would help?"
  - "What's a skill you want to develop in the next quarter?"
- **Common 1:1 mistakes:**
  - Canceling or rescheduling repeatedly (signals: "You're not a priority")
  - Doing all the talking (this is THEIR meeting)
  - Only discussing tasks and deadlines (misses the human dimension)

<!-- notes: Have participants draft 3 questions they'll bring to their next 1:1. For those who don't have regular 1:1s yet, ask them to schedule one this week. The 1:1 is where trust is built and problems are caught early -- it's the single most impactful habit for a new manager. -->

## Giving Technical Feedback Without Taking Over

- The temptation: you see a code review and you want to rewrite it yourself
- The discipline: coach them to the answer instead
- **The SBI Model for technical feedback (Center for Creative Leadership):**
  - **Situation:** "In yesterday's PR for the authentication service..."
  - **Behavior:** "I noticed the error handling doesn't account for the timeout scenario..."
  - **Impact:** "If that edge case hits in production, it could cause cascading failures for downstream services..."
- **Then hand it back:** "How would you approach fixing that?" (not "Here's what I'd do")
- **The 80% rule:** If their solution is 80% as good as what you'd have done, let it ship. The 20% gap is the cost of development -- and it's worth it
- **When to step in directly:** Production emergencies, security vulnerabilities, architectural decisions with long-term consequences

> Your job is not to write the best code. Your job is to build a team that writes great code without you.

<!-- notes: The 80% rule is liberating for perfectionist engineers. It explicitly gives them permission to accept "good enough" in service of team growth. The exceptions list is important too -- it prevents the pendulum from swinging too far toward hands-off. -->

## Pair Activity: The Coaching Conversation

- **Setup (2 min):** Partner A is a new engineering manager. Partner B is a senior engineer who just submitted a PR with a suboptimal database query approach. It works, but it won't scale.
- **Round 1 (3 min):** Partner A gives feedback using the "old way" -- telling the engineer what's wrong and how to fix it
- **Round 2 (3 min):** Partner A gives feedback using the coaching approach -- asking questions to help the engineer discover the issue themselves
- **Debrief questions:**
  - How did it feel to receive feedback each way?
  - Which approach is more likely to build the engineer's capability for next time?
  - When is the "telling" approach appropriate despite its limitations?

<!-- notes: This exercise is the emotional core of the module. Engineers who become managers default to telling because it's efficient. The role-play shows them the difference in experience and outcome. Allow 10 minutes total. Debrief as a full group for 2-3 minutes. -->

## Managing Your Former Peers

- One of the most awkward aspects of the IC-to-manager transition
- **The reality:** Yesterday you were equals; today you have positional authority over them
- **What changes:**
  - You can no longer vent about the organization to them as freely
  - You'll have information you can't share (compensation, performance issues, company plans)
  - You need to give them feedback and evaluate their performance
  - Some may resent the change or test boundaries
- **How to navigate it:**
  - **Address it directly:** "Our relationship is going to change. I want to be transparent about that."
  - **Earn authority, don't assert it:** Lead through competence and care, not positional power
  - **Be fair, not equal:** Treat everyone equitably; don't give former friends preferential treatment
  - **Accept the loneliness:** Management is lonelier than IC work. Build peer relationships with other managers.

> The loneliness of management isn't a bug -- it's a feature. The separation creates the space for objectivity and fairness.

<!-- notes: This is a sensitive topic. Many new managers lose friendships during the transition and grieve that loss. Normalize it: the relationship doesn't end, but it changes. Encourage them to build new peer relationships with other managers -- this is their new support system. -->

## Building Your New Leadership Identity

- The transition takes 6-12 months -- don't rush it
- **Actions to accelerate the shift:**
  - Schedule 1:1s religiously -- this is your primary work now
  - Block "thinking time" for team strategy and people development
  - Find a manager mentor -- someone who's navigated this transition
  - Join a peer group of other new managers
  - Read about management, not just technology
- **Keep your technical edge without doing the work:**
  - Stay in architecture reviews and design discussions
  - Read code selectively for understanding, not for contribution
  - Attend tech talks and maintain domain knowledge
  - Use technical credibility to ask better questions, not to override decisions

> You don't stop being technical. You start being technical in service of your team instead of in service of the codebase.

<!-- notes: This is a grounding slide that reassures participants they don't have to abandon their technical identity entirely. The distinction between using technical knowledge to serve the team vs. serve the codebase is the key insight. -->

## Your Personal Transition Plan

- **Reflect individually (3 min) and write down:**

  2. What is one management habit I need to start this week?
  3. Who will be my manager-mentor or accountability partner?
  4. What does "success" look like for me in 90 days -- not in code shipped, but in team outcomes?

## Key Takeaways

- The IC-to-manager transition is a **career change**, not a promotion -- treat it that way
- Your new output metric is your **team's output**, not your personal output
- **Coaching over telling:** Build your team's capability, not their dependency on you
- The **80% rule:** If their solution is 80% as good as yours, let it ship
- **1:1s are your primary work** -- never skip them, never rush them
- The transition takes **6-12 months** -- be patient with yourself, but be intentional
- **Leverage, not effort:** Always ask, "Is this the highest-leverage use of my time?"

> You were promoted because you were an excellent engineer. You'll stay promoted because you become an excellent leader. Those are different skills -- and the learning starts now.
