## Why Cross-Cultural Leadership Matters

- Global teams are the norm, not the exception
- Cultural misunderstandings are the hidden tax on international collaboration
- What looks like "difficult behavior" is often a cultural difference
- Leaders who can bridge cultures unlock diverse perspectives and outperform monocultural teams
- The goal isn't to become an expert in every culture -- it's to develop cultural curiosity and adaptive skill

> Culture is the invisible water we swim in. The first step is noticing it exists.

## Cultural Dimensions That Shape Leadership

- Based on Hofstede and Erin Meyer's research:
  - **Power Distance:** How comfortable are people with hierarchy? (Flat vs. hierarchical)
  - **Communication Style:** Direct vs. indirect -- is meaning in the words or between them?
  - **Time Orientation:** Linear (punctuality, sequential) vs. flexible (relationships, multi-tasking)
  - **Individualism/Collectivism:** "I" achievement vs. "we" harmony -- how are decisions made?
  - **Uncertainty Avoidance:** Comfort with ambiguity vs. need for rules and structure
- No "right" culture -- only different defaults shaped by history, geography, and social norms

<!-- notes: For each dimension, ask participants to place themselves and their team on the spectrum. The insight: even within one country, there's significant variation. Dimensions are tendencies, not stereotypes. -->

## Cross-Cultural Friction Points

- Common misunderstandings that derail international collaboration:
  - **Direct feedback** -- valued as honest in some cultures, perceived as rude in others
  - **Silence after proposals** -- agreement in some contexts, disagreement in others
  - **Missed deadlines** -- irresponsible in linear cultures, flexible prioritization in others
  - **Decisions without meetings** -- efficient in low power distance, disrespectful in high power distance
  - **Personal questions** -- relationship building in collectivist cultures, intrusive in individualist ones
- The bridge skill: always ask "Is this a personality issue or a cultural difference?"

## Turkey in Context

- Higher power distance than Northern Europe -- respect for hierarchy matters
- High-context communication -- what's NOT said often matters more than what IS said
- Strong collective loyalty -- relationships and group harmony are prioritized
- Moderate uncertainty avoidance with entrepreneurial flexibility
- Hospitality and relationship-first business culture -- trust is built personally before professionally
- Bridging position between European and Middle Eastern business cultures

## Leveraging Cultural Diversity

- Diverse teams outperform homogeneous teams -- but only when well-led
- **Practices for culturally intelligent leadership:**
  - Make cultural norms explicit rather than assumed
  - Create space for different communication styles (written and verbal, real-time and async)
  - Rotate facilitation and decision-making approaches
  - Build shared team norms while respecting individual cultural preferences
  - Treat cultural friction as data, not as personal conflict

> Cultural diversity isn't a challenge to manage -- it's an advantage to leverage.
