# Leading Hybrid & Remote Teams
## Building Trust, Inclusion & Rhythm Without Proximity

<!-- notes: Addresses the unique challenges of leading when you can't rely on physical presence. Builds trust and inclusion without proximity, and designs effective async/sync communication rhythms. -->

---

## The Hybrid Leadership Challenge

- What changes when you can't see your team:
  - Casual check-ins must become intentional
  - Output-based trust replaces visible effort
  - Proximity bias unconsciously favors in-person people
  - Information flows unevenly -- hallway conversations exclude remote team members
  - Loneliness and disconnection become real retention risks
- The fundamental shift: from managing presence to managing outcomes

> If you're measuring leadership by who's in the office, you're measuring the wrong thing.

<!-- notes: Ask: "How many of you have noticed that people who are physically present get more visibility, more opportunities, or more trust?" This is proximity bias -- and it's the #1 threat to hybrid team equity. -->

---

## Async-First Operating Rhythm

- Default to asynchronous communication; reserve synchronous time for what truly requires real-time interaction
- **A sample weekly rhythm:**
  - **Monday:** Written team update (async) -- priorities, blockers, wins
  - **Tuesday-Thursday:** Deep work blocks protected; async collaboration via shared documents
  - **Wednesday:** One synchronous team meeting (30-45 min) -- connection, decisions, alignment
  - **Friday:** Individual 1:1s and reflection
- **Synchronous time is precious** -- use it for decisions, difficult conversations, brainstorming, and connection
- **Asynchronous is default** -- use it for updates, reviews, information sharing, and documentation

<!-- notes: The key principle: synchronous time should be reserved for what async can't do well -- human connection, real-time problem solving, and nuanced discussions. Most teams over-index on meetings and under-invest in written communication. -->

## Inclusion Practices for Hybrid Teams

- **The "one remote, all remote" rule:** If one person is remote, everyone joins virtually -- this levels the playing field
- **Rotate meeting times** across time zones so the same people aren't always inconvenienced
- **Assign a "remote advocate"** in key meetings to watch for remote participants being talked over
- **Document decisions in writing** -- what isn't written down didn't happen
- **Create informal connection points:** Virtual coffee chats, shared interest channels, beginning meetings with personal check-ins

<!-- notes: The documentation point is critical: in hybrid teams, the biggest source of inequity is information that gets shared in hallways and never documented. Make writing a cultural norm, not an afterthought. -->

---

## Avoiding Common Hybrid Pitfalls

- **Proximity bias:** Giving better assignments, more visibility, or faster promotions to in-office staff
- **Meeting overload:** Compensating for distance with more meetings instead of better async practices
- **Surveillance mindset:** Monitoring activity rather than measuring outcomes
- **Culture erosion:** Assuming culture will maintain itself without intentional effort
- **Loneliness epidemic:** Ignoring the mental health impact of remote isolation
- The antidote: intentional design, regular audits of equity, and asking your team what's working

> The best hybrid leaders don't manage remote work -- they design it.

<!-- notes: Close with a self-audit: "Review your last 5 project assignments, promotions, or growth opportunities. Were remote team members represented proportionally?" If not, proximity bias may be at work. -->
