# The Strategic Leader
## Ownership, Delegation, Decision-Making & Change

<!-- notes: Accountability, ownership, and effective delegation are the foundation of effective leadership. This module addresses how leaders take full responsibility for outcomes, delegate to develop capability, make decisions in complexity, and drive change. -->

---

## Why Strategy Matters for Every Leader

- Strategy is not reserved for the C-suite -- every leader makes strategic choices daily
- **What "strategic" means at your level:**
  - Allocating your team's limited time and attention to highest-impact work
  - Making trade-off decisions that align with organizational priorities
  - Anticipating challenges before they become crises
  - Connecting your team's work to the broader organizational mission
- **The strategic leadership gap:** Most leaders are promoted for execution, not for strategy. The shift from "getting things done" to "deciding what should get done" is the hardest transition.
- **Henry Mintzberg's research** distinguishes between deliberate strategy (planned) and emergent strategy (adaptive). Effective leaders do both.

> A leader without strategic thinking is just a very busy person. Strategy is the difference between motion and progress.

<!-- notes: Open by challenging the assumption that strategy is someone else's job. Ask participants: "What strategic decisions have you made in the past month?" Many will struggle to name any -- which illustrates the gap. The Mintzberg reference grounds the concept in research and legitimizes the reality that strategy emerges from daily decisions, not just annual planning sessions. -->

## Extreme Ownership & Accountability

- From Jocko Willink & Leif Babin: Leaders must own everything in their world. There is no one else to blame.
- **The Ownership Mindset:**
  - "What could I have done differently?" vs. "It wasn't my fault"
  - "I'm responsible for the outcome" vs. "I did my part"
  - "How can I enable my team to succeed?" vs. "My team let me down"
- What it looks like: own the outcome not just the task, no excuses, own your team's failures, support other teams, and lead both up and down
- Critical distinction: **Accountability is forward-looking** ("How do we fix this?"), **blame is backward-looking** ("Whose fault is this?")

> The more you own, the more you grow. The more you blame, the more you stay stuck.

<!-- notes: This is often a confrontational slide for participants. Ask: "Think of a recent failure. What's your instinct -- to explain or to own?" The shift from explaining to owning is one of the hardest and most important leadership transitions. -->

## The Accountability Ladder

- Not all accountability is equal -- leaders operate at different levels
- **Roger Connors and Tom Smith's "The Oz Principle" -- The Accountability Ladder:**
  - **Below the line (victim mindset):**
    1. Unaware / Unconscious -- "I didn't know"
    2. Blame others -- "It's not my fault"
    3. Excuses -- "I can't because..."
    4. Wait and hope -- "Maybe it will work out"
  - **Above the line (ownership mindset):**
    5. Acknowledge reality -- "Here's what happened"
    6. Own it -- "Here's my role in this"
    7. Find solutions -- "Here's what I can do"
    8. Make it happen -- "Here's what I'm doing now"
- **The leader's job:** Model above-the-line behavior consistently, AND create a team culture where above-the-line is the norm
- **Self-check:** Where on the ladder is your default response when something goes wrong?

> The line between victim and owner isn't about circumstances -- it's about the story you tell yourself about those circumstances.

<!-- notes: The Accountability Ladder gives participants a concrete self-assessment tool. Have them think of a current challenge and identify where they are on the ladder. Then ask: "What would it take to move one step higher?" The visual of above/below the line is powerful and easy to reference in future conversations. -->

## Exercise: The Ownership Audit

- **Step 1 (3 min, individual):** Identify a recent situation where something went wrong on your team or in your organization.
- **Step 2 (3 min):** Write down honest answers to these questions:
  - What was my initial reaction? (Above or below the line?)
  - What role did I play in the outcome -- even indirectly?
  - What could I have done differently to prevent or mitigate the issue?
  - What did I learn that I can apply going forward?
- **Step 3 (4 min, pairs):** Share your reflection with a partner. The partner's job is NOT to console or agree -- it's to gently push: "Is there anything else you could have owned?"
- **The growth edge:** Ownership gets uncomfortable precisely at the point where it becomes transformative

<!-- notes: This exercise requires psychological safety. Emphasize that the purpose is growth, not guilt. The partner's role as a gentle challenger is important -- it prevents the exercise from becoming a self-congratulatory retelling. Allow 10 minutes total. -->

## The Delegating Leader -- Skill/Will Matrix

- Leaders fail to delegate for predictable reasons: "It's faster if I do it myself," "No one can do it as well as me," "I don't want to overload my team"
- The cost: you become the bottleneck, your team doesn't grow, you burn out
- **Match delegation approach to the person:**
  - **Direct** (low skill, low will) -- clear instructions, monitoring
  - **Motivate** (low skill, high will) -- teach, coach, encourage
  - **Challenge** (high skill, low will) -- investigate, stretch
  - **Delegate** (high skill, high will) -- autonomy, outcomes not methods
- **The delegation conversation:** What (clear outcome), Why (purpose), Boundaries (authority), Support (resources), Check-in (follow-up, not micromanagement)

<!-- notes: Have participants identify one thing they should delegate this week and determine which quadrant the recipient falls into. The most common mistake: treating everyone the same way regardless of skill and will. -->

## The Delegation Spectrum

- Delegation is not binary (do it yourself vs. hand it off completely) -- it's a spectrum
- **Seven levels of delegation (Jurgen Appelo, "Management 3.0"):**
  1. **Tell:** You make the decision and announce it
  2. **Sell:** You make the decision and explain why
  3. **Consult:** You ask for input, then decide
  4. **Agree:** You decide together as equals
  5. **Advise:** You offer your opinion, they decide
  6. **Inquire:** They decide and inform you
  7. **Delegate:** They decide; you don't need to know
- **The key question:** For each task or decision, what's the right level of delegation given the person's skill/will and the stakes involved?
- **Common errors:**
  - Defaulting to Level 1-2 (micromanagement) -- creates dependency
  - Jumping to Level 7 (full delegation) too quickly -- creates risk
  - Not being explicit about which level you're operating at -- creates confusion

> The best delegators are explicit about HOW they're delegating, not just WHAT they're delegating.

<!-- notes: The seven-level model gives participants nuance that the simple "delegate or don't" framework lacks. Have them pick a current task and identify the right delegation level. Then ask: "Have you communicated that level to the person?" Most haven't -- and the mismatch in expectations causes friction. -->

## Exercise: The Delegation Decision

- **Step 1 (3 min, individual):** List 5 tasks or decisions currently on your plate
- **Step 2 (3 min):** For each, answer:
  - Should I be doing this, or should someone else? (Ownership question)
  - If someone else, who? What's their skill/will quadrant for this task?
  - What delegation level (1-7) is appropriate?
  - What support do they need to succeed?
- **Step 3 (4 min, pairs):** Share your delegation plan. Partner challenges:
  - "Are you sure you need to be involved in that one?"
  - "Could you delegate at a higher level than you've chosen?"
  - "What's the worst that happens if you let go?"
- **Commitment:** Identify one task to delegate this week. Write down the delegation conversation you'll have.

<!-- notes: The partner challenge is designed to push participants toward more delegation, since the natural bias is to retain control. The "What's the worst that happens?" question often reveals that the consequences of letting go are much smaller than feared. Allow 10 minutes total. -->

## Strategic Decision-Making -- The RAPID Framework

- One common failure: unclear decision rights
- **RAPID clarifies roles:**
  - **R**ecommend -- gather data, analyze, propose
  - **A**gree -- must agree for decision to proceed
  - **P**erform -- implement the decision
  - **I**nput -- consulted, provide perspective
  - **D**ecide -- makes the final call
- **Key biases to watch:**
  - Confirmation Bias -- seek disconfirming evidence
  - Sunk Cost Fallacy -- evaluate future value only
  - Groupthink -- assign devil's advocate
  - Authority Bias -- create safe challenge culture

<!-- notes: Apply RAPID to a current decision in participants' organizations. The most common discovery: nobody is clear on who has the "D" -- the actual decision-making authority. This ambiguity is a top source of organizational frustration. -->

## Decision-Making Under Uncertainty

- Most important decisions are made with incomplete information -- waiting for certainty is itself a decision (often the wrong one)
- **Jeff Bezos's Type 1 / Type 2 Decision Framework:**
  - **Type 1 (one-way door):** Irreversible, high-stakes decisions. Take your time, gather data, involve others.
  - **Type 2 (two-way door):** Reversible, lower-stakes decisions. Decide quickly, iterate, course-correct.
  - **The common mistake:** Treating Type 2 decisions like Type 1 -- applying heavy process to decisions that can easily be reversed. This creates organizational paralysis.
- **The 70% Rule (attributed to Colin Powell):** Make the decision when you have 70% of the information you wish you had. Below 70%, you're guessing. Above 70%, you're too slow.
- **Decision velocity matters:** In fast-moving environments, the cost of a slow good decision often exceeds the cost of a fast adequate one

> The goal of decision-making is not to be right every time. It's to make good decisions quickly and correct course when needed.

<!-- notes: The Bezos framework is immediately practical. Have participants classify their three most important current decisions as Type 1 or Type 2. Most will discover they're applying Type 1 rigor to Type 2 decisions -- the most common organizational decision-making failure. The 70% rule gives them a heuristic for when to stop analyzing and start acting. -->

## Cognitive Biases in Decision-Making

- **Daniel Kahneman's research** demonstrates that even experienced leaders make systematic errors in judgment
- **Six biases every leader must recognize:**
  1. **Anchoring:** The first piece of information disproportionately influences the decision. Counter: explicitly generate multiple starting points.
  2. **Availability Bias:** Recent or vivid events seem more likely than they are. Counter: base decisions on data, not recent experiences.
  3. **Confirmation Bias:** We seek information that confirms what we already believe. Counter: assign someone to argue the opposite case.
  4. **Sunk Cost Fallacy:** We continue investing in failing efforts because of past investment. Counter: ask "Would I start this today if I weren't already committed?"
  5. **Groupthink:** Teams converge on consensus without critically evaluating alternatives. Counter: use structured dissent (pre-mortem, devil's advocate).
  6. **Overconfidence:** We overestimate our ability to predict outcomes. Counter: track decision outcomes and compare to predictions.
- **The leader's role:** You don't need to eliminate biases -- you need to create processes that compensate for them

<!-- notes: Walk through each bias with a brief example from organizational life. The counters are the actionable takeaway -- participants should pick 2-3 biases they're most prone to and implement the corresponding counter. The overconfidence bias is particularly relevant for senior leaders who have a track record of success. -->

## The Pre-Mortem Technique

- **Gary Klein's Pre-Mortem** is one of the most powerful decision-quality tools available
- **How it works:**
  1. The team assumes the decision has been implemented and has **failed spectacularly**
  2. Each person independently writes down the reasons for the failure (2-3 minutes)
  3. The team shares and discusses all failure scenarios
  4. The decision plan is revised to mitigate the most likely failure modes
- **Why it works:**
  - Overcomes groupthink: it's psychologically safe to predict failure when everyone is doing it
  - Overcomes overconfidence: forcing failure thinking counteracts the planning fallacy
  - Surfaces hidden concerns: people voice worries they wouldn't raise in a normal planning meeting
- **When to use it:** Before any significant decision, investment, or change initiative

> The pre-mortem asks the question everyone is thinking but no one is asking: "What could go wrong?"

<!-- notes: If time allows, run a mini pre-mortem on a real decision from a participant's organization. The exercise takes 5-7 minutes and the insights are always valuable. Participants are consistently surprised by how many failure modes they identify -- failure modes that were invisible in the normal planning process. -->

## Leading Change in Institutions

- **The Change Equation: D x V x F > R**
  - **D**issatisfaction with status quo
  - **V**ision of a better future
  - **F**irst achievable steps
  - Must exceed **R**esistance
  - If any factor is zero, change fails
- Understanding resistance -- it's not opposition, it's information:
  - Loss of control: "This is happening TO me"
  - Uncertainty: "What does this mean for me?"
  - Competence concerns: "I'm not sure I can do this"
  - Past failures: "We've been burned before"
- Coalition building: start with the "movable middle," find champions with credibility, create early wins, address skeptics directly

> Resistance isn't opposition -- it's information.

<!-- notes: Have participants apply the change equation to a current change initiative. Which factor is weakest? That's where they need to focus. The reframe on resistance is crucial -- most leaders treat resistance as the enemy rather than as diagnostic data. -->

## Kotter's 8-Step Change Model

- **John Kotter's research at Harvard** identified why 70% of organizational change efforts fail -- and the eight steps that distinguish success:
  1. **Create urgency:** Help people see the need for change and the cost of inaction
  2. **Build a guiding coalition:** Assemble a group with enough power, credibility, and expertise to lead the change
  3. **Form a strategic vision:** Clarify what the future looks like and how it differs from the present
  4. **Enlist a volunteer army:** Communicate the vision broadly and get buy-in from the wider organization
  5. **Enable action by removing barriers:** Eliminate obstacles that prevent people from acting on the vision
  6. **Generate short-term wins:** Plan and execute visible improvements early to build momentum
  7. **Sustain acceleration:** Use early wins to fuel bigger changes; don't declare victory too early
  8. **Institute change:** Anchor new approaches in the organizational culture
- **The most common failure point:** Step 1 -- leaders skip urgency because they assume everyone sees the need. They don't.

> Most change efforts fail not because the plan is wrong, but because Step 1 was skipped. Without urgency, nothing else matters.

<!-- notes: Kotter's model is a roadmap, not a linear sequence -- leaders often need to work on multiple steps simultaneously. The key teaching point is the failure at Step 1: many leaders are so convinced of the need for change that they forget to build that conviction in others. Ask participants: "For your current change initiative, how strong is the sense of urgency among the people who need to change?" -->

## The Human Side of Change

- Change models describe the process; but leaders must also navigate the emotional journey
- **William Bridges' Transition Model** distinguishes between change (external) and transition (internal):
  - **Ending:** Letting go of the old way. People experience loss, grief, resistance. Acknowledge it.
  - **Neutral Zone:** The uncomfortable in-between. Confusion, anxiety, low productivity. Normalize it.
  - **New Beginning:** Embracing the new way. Energy, identity, purpose. Celebrate it.
- **Leader's role at each stage:**
  - Ending: Validate what's being lost. "I understand this is hard. What you built mattered."
  - Neutral Zone: Provide structure and patience. "We're in the messy middle. That's normal."
  - New Beginning: Reinforce purpose and early wins. "Look at what we've already accomplished together."
- **The mistake:** Focusing only on the destination (new beginning) while ignoring the journey (ending and neutral zone)

> People don't resist change. They resist loss. Address the loss, and the change becomes possible.

<!-- notes: Bridges' model adds the emotional dimension that Kotter's model lacks. The distinction between change and transition is powerful: the org chart can change overnight, but people's internal adjustment takes months. Ask: "What are people losing in your current change initiative?" The answers reveal where to focus emotional support. -->

## Exercise: Change Initiative Analysis

- **Step 1 (3 min, individual):** Identify a change initiative you're currently leading or involved in
- **Step 2 (5 min):** Analyze it using two frameworks:
  - **The Change Equation:** Rate each factor (D, V, F, R) on a 1-10 scale. Which is weakest?
  - **Kotter's 8 Steps:** Which step are you currently on? Which step have you skipped or underinvested in?
  - **Bridges' Transitions:** Where are most people -- Ending, Neutral Zone, or New Beginning? Are you meeting them where they are?
- **Step 3 (5 min, pairs):** Share your analysis with a partner. Discuss:
  - What's the single biggest risk to this change initiative?
  - What one action would most increase the probability of success?
- **Step 4:** Write down your one action and commit to taking it this week

<!-- notes: This exercise synthesizes the three change models into a practical diagnostic. The combination is powerful: the Change Equation identifies what's missing, Kotter identifies what's been skipped, and Bridges identifies where people are emotionally. Allow 13 minutes total. The one-action commitment ensures the analysis translates into behavior. -->

## Communication During Change

- Change fails or succeeds based on communication -- not one big announcement, but ongoing dialogue
- **The 7x Rule:** People need to hear a message **seven times** in different ways before it sinks in. Most leaders communicate once and wonder why nothing changes.
- **Three communication channels during change:**
  1. **Town halls and all-hands:** Set the vision and the urgency (reaches everyone, limited depth)
  2. **Small group conversations:** Address concerns, answer questions, build understanding (moderate reach, deeper engagement)
  3. **1:1 conversations:** Understand individual fears, address personal impact, build commitment (limited reach, deepest engagement)
- **What to communicate:**
  - The "why" (urgency and vision) -- early and often
  - The "what" (specific changes) -- as soon as it's clear
  - The "how" (process and timeline) -- with room for iteration
  - The "what it means for you" (personal impact) -- individually, not generically
- **The most important message:** "I don't have all the answers yet, and I'll share what I know as soon as I can." Honesty builds trust; false certainty destroys it.

> In change communication, silence is never neutral. When leaders don't communicate, people fill the void with fear.

<!-- notes: The 7x rule is usually eye-opening -- most leaders dramatically underestimate the amount of communication required during change. Ask: "How many times have you communicated your current change vision?" If the answer is less than 7, they haven't communicated enough. The three-channel approach ensures both breadth and depth of communication. -->

## Group Activity: Change Communication Draft

- **Form groups of 3-4 people**
- **Choose one participant's real change initiative**
- **Draft three communications (10 min total):**
  1. **The all-hands message (3 min):** 60-second opening for a town hall that creates urgency and paints the vision. Focus on the "why."
  2. **The small-group talking points (3 min):** Five key questions people will ask, with honest answers (including "We don't know yet" where appropriate)
  3. **The 1:1 conversation guide (4 min):** Three questions to ask each direct report to understand their personal concerns and how the change affects them
- **Debrief:** Each group shares their all-hands opening with the full room. Feedback:
  - Did it create urgency without creating panic?
  - Was the vision clear and compelling?
  - Would you follow this leader through the change?

<!-- notes: This activity brings the change communication principles to life through practice. The three-tier structure (all-hands, small group, 1:1) reinforces that effective change communication requires multiple channels and levels of depth. The debrief of the all-hands opening provides immediate feedback on communication effectiveness. Allow 15 minutes total. -->

## Sustaining Change: The First 100 Days

- Most change initiatives lose momentum between days 30 and 90 -- the "messy middle" where initial excitement fades and the hard work begins
- **Sustaining momentum:**
  - **Quick wins (Days 1-30):** Identify and execute 2-3 visible improvements. These prove that change is possible and build credibility.
  - **Deepen engagement (Days 31-60):** Move beyond the early adopters. Engage the "movable middle" through small-group conversations and peer champions.
  - **Address resistance (Days 31-60):** Don't avoid skeptics -- seek them out. Their concerns often contain important information.
  - **Measure and share progress (Days 61-90):** Create a visible dashboard of change metrics. Celebrate progress publicly.
  - **Embed in systems (Days 61-100):** Change that depends on individual effort won't last. Embed new behaviors in processes, incentives, and norms.
- **The leader's energy management:** Change is exhausting for the leader too. Pace yourself, share the load, and replenish your own energy.

> Change doesn't fail because people don't want it. It fails because leaders don't sustain it through the messy middle.

<!-- notes: The 100-day framework provides a practical roadmap that bridges the gap between "we announced the change" and "the change is embedded." The emphasis on the messy middle (days 30-90) is where most change efforts die -- not from opposition, but from neglect. The self-care point at the end is important: burned-out change leaders create burned-out organizations. -->

## Integration: The Strategic Leadership Model

- The four competencies of this module work together as a system:
- **Ownership** provides the foundation: you can't delegate, decide, or lead change without first owning the outcome
- **Delegation** creates capacity: you can't think strategically if you're buried in tasks others could do
- **Decision-making** provides direction: clear, timely decisions keep organizations moving
- **Change leadership** drives transformation: the ability to take organizations from where they are to where they need to be
- **The virtuous cycle:**
  - Ownership builds trust -> Trust enables delegation -> Delegation creates capacity for strategic thinking -> Strategic thinking improves decision quality -> Good decisions drive successful change -> Successful change builds more trust
- **The vicious cycle:**
  - Blame erodes trust -> Low trust prevents delegation -> No delegation creates overwhelm -> Overwhelm degrades decisions -> Bad decisions undermine change -> Failed change reinforces blame

> Strategic leadership is not four separate skills. It's one integrated capability: the ability to own outcomes, develop people, make decisions, and drive change -- simultaneously.

<!-- notes: This integration slide is the conceptual capstone of the module. The virtuous/vicious cycle visualization shows participants that these four competencies are interconnected -- strength in one reinforces the others, and weakness in one undermines the others. Ask: "Which part of the cycle is weakest for you? That's where your development effort will have the highest return." -->

## Key Takeaways

- **Own everything:** Accountability is the foundation of leadership. Move above the line on the Accountability Ladder.
- **Delegate with precision:** Use the Skill/Will Matrix and the 7-Level Delegation Spectrum to match your approach to the person and the situation.
- **Decide with discipline:** Use RAPID for clarity, the Type 1/Type 2 framework for speed, and the Pre-Mortem for quality.
- **Lead change with empathy:** Apply the Change Equation for diagnosis, Kotter for process, and Bridges for the human dimension.
- **Communicate relentlessly:** The 7x Rule -- if you haven't said it seven times, you haven't said it enough.
- **These four competencies form a system:** Strength in one reinforces the others. Start where you're weakest.

> Strategic leadership is not about having all the answers. It's about owning the outcome, empowering your team, making timely decisions, and navigating change with courage and humanity.
