## Why Relationships Are the Operating System of Leadership

- Leadership is not a solo act -- it happens **between** people
- Research from the Center for Creative Leadership: the #1 reason leaders derail is not lack of competence -- it is **relationship failures**
- Three relationship failures that end careers:
  1. **Inability to build a team** -- surrounding yourself with the wrong people or failing to develop trust
  2. **Inability to adapt** -- applying one style to every person and situation
  3. **Inability to manage up** -- losing the confidence of senior leadership
- Technical brilliance without relational skill creates a ceiling

> Your network is your net worth. The quality of your relationships determines the quality of your leadership.

<!-- notes: Open with the CCL data -- it surprises people. Most assume leaders fail because of strategy or competence. ## The Trust Equation

- From David Maister, Charles Green & Robert Galford's research (*The Trusted Advisor*):

**Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-Orientation**

- **Credibility:** Do people believe your expertise? Do you have a track record?
- **Reliability:** Do you do what you say you'll do? Can people count on you?
- **Intimacy:** Do people feel safe with you? Can they be vulnerable? Do you keep confidences?
- **Self-Orientation** (the denominator): Are you in it for yourself, or for the shared mission?

> You can be highly credible and reliable but still not trusted if people sense you're primarily in it for yourself.

<!-- notes: Write the equation on the flipchart. The key insight: self-orientation is the DENOMINATOR. Even a small increase in self-orientation dramatically reduces trust. Conversely, even modest credibility and reliability yield high trust if self-orientation is low. Ask: "Which dimension is your strongest? Which is your weakest?" -->

## Cohen & Bradford's Influence Without Authority

- Allan Cohen and David Bradford's model from *Influence Without Authority* -- essential for matrixed organizations:
- **Core principle:** All influence is based on the **law of reciprocity** -- people give what they get
- The process:
  1. **Assume all people are potential allies** -- approach with curiosity, not suspicion
  2. **Clarify your goals and priorities** -- know what you need before you ask
  3. **Diagnose their world** -- understand their pressures, goals, fears, and currencies
  4. **Identify relevant currencies** -- what do they value that you can offer?
  5. **Deal with the relationship** -- address trust and relationship quality before pushing your agenda
  6. **Influence through give and take** -- make exchanges that serve both parties

<!-- notes: The "currencies" concept is the breakthrough here. Currencies are what people value -- and they differ widely. Some people value recognition, others value information, others value autonomy, others value being included. The leader who understands someone's currencies can influence without authority. -->

## Currencies of Influence

- People are motivated by different **currencies** -- understanding someone's currency is the key to influence:

- **Inspiration-related:** Vision, meaning, moral purpose, excellence
- **Task-related:** Resources, support, information, rapid response, technical assistance
- **Position-related:** Recognition, visibility, reputation, advancement, access to decision-makers
- **Relationship-related:** Inclusion, belonging, personal support, understanding
- **Personal-related:** Gratitude, ownership, self-worth, autonomy, challenge

- **The mistake:** Offering YOUR currency to someone who values a DIFFERENT one
- **The skill:** Diagnosing what someone values and offering that -- even when it differs from what you value

<!-- notes: Give an example: "If you value recognition but your colleague values autonomy, publicly praising them might actually feel uncomfortable. They'd prefer you to give them more decision-making freedom." Ask participants to think about a key person they need to influence: "What is THEIR currency?" This reframing -- from "how do I get what I want" to "what do they need" -- is the foundation of ethical influence. -->

## Stakeholder Mapping: The Power-Interest Grid

- For any initiative or challenge, you need a clear map of the stakeholder landscape
- **Step 1 -- Identify all stakeholders:**
  - Who is directly affected?
  - Who has decision-making authority?
  - Who has **informal** influence (often more important than formal authority)?
  - Who controls resources?
  - Who could block progress?
  - Who is affected but has no voice?

<!-- notes: Emphasize the last bullet -- stakeholders who are affected but have no voice are often overlooked and can become resisters if not engaged. Also highlight informal influence: in many organizations, the person with the most influence is not the most senior person -- it's the trusted advisor, the long-tenured expert, or the culture carrier. -->

---

## The Power-Interest Grid

- Plot each stakeholder on the grid:

| | **Low Interest** | **High Interest** |
|---|---|---|
| **High Power** | **Keep Satisfied** -- consult and inform | **Manage Closely** -- collaborate, co-create |
| **Low Power** | **Monitor** -- inform as needed | **Keep Informed** -- regular updates, listen |

- **Manage Closely (high power, high interest):** Your most critical stakeholders -- they can make or break your initiative
- **Keep Satisfied (high power, low interest):** Dangerous if ignored -- they can suddenly engage and derail
- **Keep Informed (low power, high interest):** Your advocates and early adopters -- mobilize their energy
- **Monitor (low power, low interest):** Don't over-invest, but don't ignore -- circumstances change

<!-- notes: The most common mistake is ignoring "Keep Satisfied" stakeholders -- senior leaders who seem disengaged but can suddenly veto your initiative. The second most common mistake: spending all your time managing upward (Manage Closely) and neglecting your advocates (Keep Informed) who could be amplifying your message. -->

## Moving Stakeholders: From Blocker to Supporter

- Stakeholders are not static -- you can move them
- **Strategies for each position:**
  - **Blocker to Neutral:** Address their specific concern, find common ground, reduce their perceived risk
  - **Neutral to Supporter:** Show them what's in it for them, involve them early, give them a role
  - **Supporter to Champion:** Give them visibility, share credit, equip them with talking points
- **The 80/20 Rule of Stakeholder Management:** Spend 80% of your energy on the moveable middle, not the entrenched extremes
- **Never make permanent enemies** -- today's blocker is tomorrow's boss

> The art of stakeholder management is not getting everyone to agree. It is getting enough people aligned to move forward.

<!-- notes: The "never make permanent enemies" principle is especially important in organizations where people move between roles. Share an example if you have one: a person who blocked an initiative and later became a key sponsor of a different initiative. The 80/20 rule saves enormous energy -- some people will never be supporters, and that's okay. -->

## Navigating Organizational Politics

- **Politics is not a dirty word** -- it is how decisions get made and resources get allocated in every human organization
- Denial of politics does not make them go away -- it makes you naive
- **Principles for ethical political navigation:**
  1. **Understand the informal power structure** -- who really makes decisions?
  2. **Build alliances based on shared interests** -- not transactions
  3. **Manage your political capital wisely** -- don't spend it on small battles
  4. **Build coalitions before you need them** -- once you need support, it's too late to build relationships
  5. **Stay true to your values** -- political skill without integrity is manipulation

<!-- notes: This topic often generates strong reactions. Acknowledge that "politics" has a negative connotation. Reframe: all organizations are political systems. The question is whether you navigate them skillfully and ethically, or pretend they don't exist and get blindsided. Ask: "What's the difference between political skill and manipulation?" Answer: intent and transparency. -->

## Reading the Political Landscape

- **Questions to diagnose your organization's political dynamics:**
  - Who are the **real** decision-makers (not just the formal ones)?
  - What are the **unwritten rules** that everyone knows but nobody states?
  - Where is **information** actually exchanged (meetings? hallways? WhatsApp groups?)
  - Who are the **culture carriers** -- the people others look to for cues?
  - What gets **rewarded** vs. what gets **talked about**? (These are often different)
  - Who has been **promoted** recently and why? This reveals the real criteria
- **The politically aware leader** doesn't play games -- they understand the field they're playing on

<!-- notes: These questions are powerful diagnostic tools. Ask participants to answer 2-3 of them for their own organization. The gap between "what we say we value" and "what we actually reward" is often the most revealing insight. This is balcony work applied to organizational politics. -->

## Discussion: Political Dilemmas

- In groups of 3-4, discuss one of these scenarios:
  - **Scenario A:** You have information that would benefit your project, but sharing it would embarrass a colleague. What do you do?
  - **Scenario B:** Your boss's boss asks your opinion about a decision your boss made. You disagree with your boss. What do you say?
  - **Scenario C:** A peer is taking credit for work your team did. Confronting them would create conflict, but staying silent sends the wrong message. How do you handle it?
- For each scenario: What are the **political risks**? What are the **ethical boundaries**? What would you **actually do**?
- **Time: 6 minutes discussion, then share key insights**

<!-- notes: These scenarios are deliberately uncomfortable because political navigation IS uncomfortable. There are no perfect answers -- only trade-offs. During the debrief, validate the tension: "Good political navigation is not about having the right answer. It's about seeing the full landscape of consequences and making a conscious choice." -->

## Strategic Communication: Adapting to Your Audience

- One message does not fit all -- the same content must be packaged differently for different audiences
- **Four communication styles** (based on the DISC framework):
  - **Analytical:** Wants data, logic, evidence, thoroughness. Give them: analysis, options, time to process
  - **Driver:** Wants results, bottom line, speed. Give them: executive summary, timeline, clear ask
  - **Expressive:** Wants vision, stories, possibilities, energy. Give them: the big picture, narrative, enthusiasm
  - **Amiable:** Wants impact on people, collaboration, harmony. Give them: team implications, inclusion, support

> Communicate in the style the listener needs, not the style you prefer.

<!-- notes: Ask participants to identify which style they default to in communication. Then ask: "What style does your boss prefer?" The mismatch between your default and their preference is often the source of communication friction. A common example: an Analytical communicator giving a 20-slide deck to a Driver who wanted a one-page summary. -->

## The Art of the Strategic Narrative

- Facts inform, but **stories** persuade
- Every strategic communication should include:
  1. **Context:** Why this matters now (urgency and relevance)
  2. **Insight:** What we learned or discovered (the "aha")
  3. **Action:** What we propose (clear and specific)
  4. **Impact:** What changes if we succeed (paint the picture)
  5. **Ask:** What you need from the audience (specific, actionable)
- **The 30-second test:** If you cannot explain your message in 30 seconds, you don't understand it well enough
- **The so-what test:** After every point, ask yourself "so what?" -- if you can't answer, cut it

<!-- notes: Practice this with a real example: "Think of something you need to communicate to senior leadership this week. Can you deliver it in 30 seconds using Context-Insight-Action-Impact-Ask?" Give them 2 minutes to draft, then ask 1-2 volunteers to practice their 30-second pitch with the group. Provide coaching on clarity and conciseness. -->

## Managing Up: The Most Underleveraged Leadership Skill

- **Managing up is not manipulation** -- it is proactively building a productive relationship with your leader
- Why most people fail at managing up:
  - They wait to be asked instead of proactively sharing
  - They bring problems without solutions
  - They communicate in their own style, not their leader's
  - They don't understand their leader's pressures and priorities
  - They surprise their leader with bad news (bad news does not improve with age)

> The leader who waits to be asked isn't leading -- they're following. Manage up proactively.

<!-- notes: This reframe is important: managing up is a LEADERSHIP skill, not a political skill. It's about making the relationship work for both parties. Ask: "How many of you proactively manage your relationship with your boss?" Usually fewer than 30% raise hands. Then ask: "How many of you wish YOUR team managed up to YOU more effectively?" Nearly everyone raises hands. The gap between those two answers is the learning opportunity. -->

---

## The Managing Up Playbook

- **Know their world:**
  - What are their top 3 priorities this quarter?
  - What pressures are they under from THEIR boss?
  - What keeps them up at night?
  - What is their communication style preference?
- **Build the relationship:**
  - No surprises -- ever. Deliver bad news early, with context and options
  - Speak their language (data, stories, bullet points -- whatever they prefer)
  - Bring solutions, not just problems (but don't hide problems)
  - Make them look good -- their success is your success
  - Anticipate their needs before they ask
- **Manage the interaction:**
  - Prepare for every one-on-one -- have an agenda, key updates, and asks ready
  - Follow up in writing on key decisions and commitments
  - Ask for feedback regularly -- don't wait for annual reviews

<!-- notes: The "know their world" section is where most people start. If you don't understand your boss's pressures, you can't manage up effectively. Suggest a practical action: "In your next one-on-one, ask your boss: 'What are your top 3 priorities this quarter, and how can I best support them?'" This single question transforms the relationship from transactional to strategic. -->

## The Network Effect: Building Your Leadership Web

- Strong leaders maintain three types of relationships:
  - **Operational network:** People you need to get today's work done (your team, direct stakeholders)
  - **Personal network:** People who provide perspective, support, and development (mentors, peers, friends)
  - **Strategic network:** People who provide vision, sponsorship, and access (senior leaders, cross-functional peers, external contacts)
- Most leaders over-invest in operational and under-invest in strategic
- **Herminia Ibarra's research (INSEAD):** Leaders who actively build strategic networks advance further and lead more effectively -- not because of favoritism, but because they have better information, broader perspective, and more resources to draw on

<!-- notes: This framework from Herminia Ibarra is eye-opening for many participants. Ask them to estimate what percentage of their networking time goes to each category. Typical answer: 80% operational, 15% personal, 5% strategic. Challenge them: "If you only invest in operational relationships, you will be excellent at today's job but unprepared for tomorrow's." -->

## Module 2 Summary: Your Relational Leadership Toolkit

- **The Trust Equation:** Build trust through credibility, reliability, and intimacy while minimizing self-orientation
- **Influence Without Authority:** Diagnose currencies and create mutually beneficial exchanges
- **Stakeholder Mapping:** Use the Power-Interest Grid to prioritize engagement strategically
- **Political Navigation:** Understand the real power structure and navigate it ethically
- **Strategic Communication:** Adapt your style to your audience; use Context-Insight-Action-Impact-Ask
- **Managing Up:** Proactively build a productive relationship with your leader
- **Network Building:** Invest in operational, personal, AND strategic networks
