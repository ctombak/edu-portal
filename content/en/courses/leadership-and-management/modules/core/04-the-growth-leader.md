## The Leader as Developer of People

- The ultimate measure of a leader is not what they achieve -- it is **what their people achieve after they leave**
- Research from Bersin by Deloitte: organizations with strong coaching cultures have **130% higher** business performance and **39% stronger** employee engagement
- Yet most leaders spend less than **10%** of their time developing others
- The shift: from leader as **problem-solver** to leader as **capacity-builder**
- This module gives you three tools: **psychological safety** (the environment), **coaching** (the conversation), and **feedback** (the catalyst)

> The growth of the leader is measured by the growth of those they lead.

## Psychological Safety: The Foundation

- **Amy Edmondson** (Harvard Business School) defined psychological safety as: *a shared belief held by members of a team that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking*
- It does NOT mean:
  - Being nice all the time
  - Avoiding conflict
  - Lowering standards
  - Agreeing with everyone
- It DOES mean:
  - People can speak up without fear of punishment or humiliation
  - Mistakes are treated as learning opportunities, not career threats
  - Dissent is welcomed as valuable data
  - Asking for help is seen as strength, not weakness

<!-- notes: The misconception that psychological safety means "being nice" or "lowering standards" is the #1 barrier to adoption. Edmondson is clear: psychologically safe teams have HIGHER standards because people can challenge each other without fear. Unsafe teams have lower effective standards because problems are hidden. -->

## The Evidence: Why Safety Drives Performance

- **Google's Project Aristotle** (2015): Studied 180+ teams over two years. Psychological safety was the **#1 predictor** of team performance -- above all other factors
- **Edmondson's hospital research:** Teams that reported MORE errors had BETTER patient outcomes -- because they were catching and learning from mistakes, not hiding them
- **The silence problem:** In psychologically unsafe environments:
  - **85%** of employees have felt unable to raise a concern (Milliken et al.)
  - Problems compound because they are not surfaced early
  - Innovation dies because ideas are not shared
  - Talent leaves because people feel they cannot be themselves

> People don't follow leaders they fear -- they simply comply until they find an exit.

<!-- notes: The Google data is powerful because it comes from a data-driven company that expected technical skills or team composition to matter most. Instead, how the team interacted mattered more than who was on the team. The hospital research is counterintuitive and memorable: more reported errors = better outcomes. Ask: "In your team, are errors reported openly or hidden?" -->

## The Four Stages of Psychological Safety

- Timothy Clark's model (*The 4 Stages of Psychological Safety*) shows safety develops in sequence:
  1. **Inclusion Safety:** Do I belong here? Am I accepted for who I am?
  2. **Learner Safety:** Can I ask questions, make mistakes, and learn without being judged?
  3. **Contributor Safety:** Can I make a meaningful contribution? Are my ideas valued?
  4. **Challenger Safety:** Can I challenge the status quo without retaliation? Can I disagree with authority?
- Each stage must be established before the next can develop
- Most teams stall at Stage 2 or 3 -- very few reach Challenger Safety
- **Challenger Safety is where innovation lives** -- and it requires the most intentional leadership

<!-- notes: Walk through the four stages and ask participants to identify where their team currently sits. Most will say Stage 2 or 3. The critical insight: you cannot have a truly innovative team without Challenger Safety, and Challenger Safety requires the leader to explicitly invite and reward dissent. Ask: "When was the last time someone on your team openly disagreed with you? If you can't remember, you may not have Challenger Safety." -->

## Seven Practices to Build Psychological Safety

1. **Model vulnerability:** Share your own mistakes and what you learned. If you can't be vulnerable, why would your team be?
2. **Ask, then listen:** Ask for input BEFORE sharing your opinion. Once the leader speaks, most people align
3. **Respond to bad news with curiosity, not blame:** "Tell me more" is more powerful than "How did this happen?"
4. **Separate intelligent failures from preventable failures:** Intelligent failures (from experimentation) should be celebrated; preventable failures (from negligence) should be addressed
5. **Create explicit norms:** "In this team, we challenge ideas, not people" -- say it, model it, reinforce it
6. **Recognize and reward candor:** Publicly thank people who raise difficult issues or share dissenting views
7. **Debrief without blame:** After failures, ask "What did we learn?" not "Whose fault was this?"

<!-- notes: Go through each practice with a brief example. Practice #2 is particularly important: once the leader shares their opinion, most team members align rather than offer alternatives. The simple act of asking before speaking transforms the quality of team thinking. Ask: "Which of these seven practices would have the biggest impact on YOUR team?" -->

## The Fail-Fast, Learn-Faster Mindset

- Innovation requires failure -- the question is whether failures are **fast, cheap, and educational** or **slow, expensive, and hidden**
- **The Fail-Fast Framework:**
  - **Run small experiments quickly** -- test ideas with minimal investment before scaling
  - **Learn from failures openly** -- extract lessons publicly, not privately
  - **Iterate based on real feedback** -- not opinions, but data from real users or stakeholders
  - **Treat failures as data** -- "We learned that X doesn't work" is progress
  - **Stop bad ideas early** -- the courage to kill a failing initiative saves resources for better ones
- **The cultural challenge:** Most organizations say they value innovation but punish failure. The gap between stated values and actual behavior destroys psychological safety

> Fail fast doesn't mean fail carelessly. It means design experiments that generate learning quickly and cheaply.

<!-- notes: The distinction between "fail fast" and "fail carelessly" is critical. Fail-fast requires discipline: clear hypotheses, small experiments, defined success criteria, and honest evaluation. It is the opposite of reckless. Ask: "Does your organization genuinely celebrate intelligent failures? What happens to someone who tries something new and it doesn't work?" The answers reveal the real culture. -->

## Case Study: Learning from Failure

- **The scenario:** A team launched a new internal process after months of development. Six weeks in, adoption is 20%, complaints are rising, and two team members have privately told you the process doesn't work. Your boss championed the initiative.
- **Discuss in groups of 3-4:**
  1. What would happen in your organization if you surfaced this failure openly?
  2. What would a **psychologically safe** response look like?
  3. What would a **fail-fast** approach have looked like from the beginning?
  4. How do you manage the conversation **upward** with your boss?
  5. What is the cost of NOT addressing this?
- **Time: 6 minutes discussion, then group debrief**

<!-- notes: This case study integrates psychological safety, fail-fast culture, and managing up. The "managing up" element (your boss championed the initiative) adds realistic complexity. During the debrief, listen for the tension between honesty and self-preservation. The key insight: in a psychologically safe culture, surfacing the failure EARLY is rewarded because it saves time and resources. In an unsafe culture, people hide it until it's too late. -->

## The Coaching Mindset: Questions Over Answers

- In the traditional model, leaders had answers. The new model: **leaders who ask good questions outperform those who give good answers**
- Michael Bungay Stanier (*The Coaching Habit*): "Stay curious a little bit longer. Rush to action a little bit slower."
- Why coaching works:
  - Questions activate **ownership** -- the coachee owns the solution
  - Answers create **dependency** -- the coachee needs you for every decision
  - Questions develop **capability** -- the coachee learns to think, not just execute
  - Answers develop **compliance** -- the coachee learns to wait for instructions
- **The 80/20 Rule of Coaching:** In a coaching conversation, the coachee should talk 80% of the time

> Tell me and I forget. Teach me and I remember. Involve me and I learn. -- Benjamin Franklin

<!-- notes: This section challenges many experienced leaders. They built their careers on having answers, and now we're saying the answers are the problem. Acknowledge the tension: "There ARE times when you need to give answers -- urgency, safety, expertise gaps. But most of the time, you give answers out of habit, not necessity." The 80/20 rule is a good diagnostic: if you're talking more than 20% of the time in a development conversation, you're telling, not coaching. -->

## The GROW Model: A Structured Coaching Conversation

- Developed by **Sir John Whitmore** (*Coaching for Performance*), the GROW model provides structure without being rigid:

- **G -- Goal:** What do you want to achieve? What would a great outcome look like? What matters most to you here?
- **R -- Reality:** What's happening now? What have you tried? What's getting in the way? How do you feel about the current situation?
- **O -- Options:** What could you do? What else? If there were no constraints, what would you try? What has worked in similar situations?
- **W -- Will (Way Forward):** What will you do? By when? What support do you need? How will you know you've succeeded? What might get in the way?

<!-- notes: Write GROW on the flipchart. Walk through each stage with an example. The most common coaching mistake: jumping from Goal to Will without exploring Reality and Options. The magic happens in the Options stage -- asking "What else?" two or three times after the first option is stated. The first answer is usually the safe answer; the third or fourth option is usually the breakthrough. -->

---

## Powerful Coaching Questions for Each GROW Stage

- **Goal:**
  - "What would success look like?"
  - "What matters most to you about this?"
  - "If this were resolved perfectly, what would be different?"
- **Reality:**
  - "What have you already tried?"
  - "What's working, even partially?"
  - "On a scale of 1-10, where are you? What would move you one point higher?"
- **Options:**
  - "What are your options?" (then: "What else?" x3)
  - "What would you advise a friend in this situation?"
  - "What would you do if you knew you couldn't fail?"
- **Will:**
  - "What's your first step?"
  - "When will you do it?"
  - "What might get in the way, and how will you handle that?"

<!-- notes: These questions are tools. Participants don't need to memorize them all -- they need 2-3 favorites from each stage. Highlight the most powerful ones: "What else?" is the single most important coaching question. "What would you advise a friend?" creates distance and clarity. "On a scale of 1-10" makes abstract issues concrete. -->

## The Seven Essential Coaching Questions

- From Michael Bungay Stanier's *The Coaching Habit* -- seven questions that transform everyday conversations:
  1. **"What's on your mind?"** -- The kickstart question (opens the conversation)
  2. **"And what else?"** -- The best coaching question in the world (deepens exploration)
  3. **"What's the real challenge here for you?"** -- The focus question (cuts through noise)
  4. **"What do you want?"** -- The foundation question (clarifies the need)
  5. **"How can I help?"** -- The lazy question (forces them to make a specific request)
  6. **"If you're saying yes to this, what are you saying no to?"** -- The strategic question (surfaces trade-offs)
  7. **"What was most useful for you?"** -- The learning question (reinforces insight)

<!-- notes: These seven questions are deceptively simple. The power is in the discipline of asking them instead of jumping to advice. Highlight question #2 ("And what else?") -- Bungay Stanier calls it the best coaching question in the world because it always yields deeper insight. And question #5 ("How can I help?") prevents the leader from assuming what help is needed. -->

## Exercise: Live Coaching Practice -- Round 1

- **Pair up** with someone different from your previous partner
- **Roles:** Coach and Coachee (you will switch)
- **Coachee:** Present a real challenge you are facing at work (2 minutes to describe it)
- **Coach:** Use ONLY questions from the GROW model and the Seven Questions. **No advice. No suggestions. Only questions.**
- **Time: 7 minutes per round** (Coach for 7 minutes, then switch)
- **Rules for the Coach:**
  - Listen more than you speak
  - Ask "And what else?" at least three times
  - Resist the urge to share your experience or give advice
  - Trust the coachee to find their own answer

<!-- notes: This is the most important exercise in the module. Set it up carefully. The "no advice" rule is essential and will be the hardest thing for most participants. Walk the room and gently redirect coaches who slip into advice-giving: "That sounds like advice -- can you turn it into a question?" After the first round (7 minutes), call time and have them switch roles. Total exercise time: 14 minutes for both rounds. -->

## Coaching Debrief: What Did You Notice?

- As a group, let's debrief:
  - **Coachees:** What was it like to be coached with only questions?
  - **Coaches:** What was hardest about not giving advice?
  - **Everyone:** What surprised you?
- **Common discoveries:**
  - The coachee often found a better solution than the coach would have suggested
  - Not giving advice was physically uncomfortable for the coach
  - The question "And what else?" consistently unlocked deeper thinking

## When to Coach vs. When to Direct

- Coaching is not always appropriate. Use the right mode for the situation:

| Situation | Mode | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Crisis / safety issue | **Direct** | Speed and clarity matter; no time for exploration |
| Employee is new / task is new | **Teach** | They need knowledge and skill before coaching works |
| Employee is capable but stuck | **Coach** | They have the knowledge; they need thinking space |
| Employee is experienced and motivated | **Delegate** | Get out of their way; they don't need you |
| Performance issue after coaching | **Direct/Consequence** | Coaching without accountability enables underperformance |

- **The common mistake:** Coaching when you should direct (wastes time in crises) or directing when you should coach (creates dependency in development)

<!-- notes: This framework prevents the "everything is a coaching moment" trap. Sometimes you need to give clear direction. The diagnostic: does this person have the capability and just need thinking space (coach), or do they lack knowledge/skill (teach), or is there urgency (direct)? Ask: "Which mode do you default to? Which do you underuse?" Most leaders default to Direct and underuse Coach. -->

## Feedback That Works: Why Most Feedback Fails

- **The feedback paradox:** 65% of employees want more feedback, yet 72% say feedback they receive is not useful (Zenger Folkman)
- Why feedback fails:
  - **Too vague:** "Good job" or "You need to improve" -- no actionable information
  - **Too late:** Feedback given months after the event has lost its connection
  - **Too personal:** Attacking character rather than describing behavior
  - **Too one-directional:** Monologue rather than dialogue
  - **Too infrequent:** Saved for annual reviews instead of given in the moment
- Poorly delivered feedback triggers **identity threat** -- the brain processes it as a social attack (Naomi Eisenberger, UCLA research on social pain)

<!-- notes: The neuroscience is important: social pain (rejection, criticism) activates the same brain regions as physical pain. This is why feedback feels threatening even when it's well-intentioned. Understanding this biology helps leaders deliver feedback more skillfully. Ask: "Think of the worst feedback you ever received. What made it so bad?" You'll hear themes of vagueness, timing, and personal attack. -->

## The SBI Framework: Situation-Behavior-Impact

- The **SBI Framework** (Center for Creative Leadership) works for BOTH corrective and reinforcing feedback:

- **S -- Situation:** Specific time and place. Grounds the feedback in a concrete moment
  - "In yesterday's team meeting..."
- **B -- Behavior:** Observable actions. What they DID, not who they ARE
  - "...when you interrupted Maria twice while she was presenting..."
- **I -- Impact:** The effect on you, the team, or the work. Explains WHY it matters
  - "...it shut down her contribution and I noticed others stopped speaking up as well."

- **Then add:** Your intent ("I'm sharing this because I value your leadership in meetings") and an invitation ("What's your perspective on this?")

<!-- notes: Walk through the full example. Emphasize: Behavior is what a video camera would capture -- observable, specific, factual. NOT "you were rude" (interpretation) but "you interrupted twice" (behavior). The Impact statement is what makes feedback meaningful -- without it, people don't understand why the behavior matters. The invitation ("What's your perspective?") turns monologue into dialogue. -->

---

## SBI in Practice: Corrective and Reinforcing Examples

- **Corrective feedback example:**
  - **S:** "In the client meeting on Tuesday..."
  - **B:** "...you presented the timeline without mentioning the risks we discussed..."
  - **I:** "...the client committed to a deadline that may not be realistic, which could damage our credibility when we miss it."
  - **Invitation:** "I want us to be transparent with clients. What was your thinking?"

- **Reinforcing feedback example:**
  - **S:** "In the project review this morning..."
  - **B:** "...you acknowledged Priya's contribution to the analysis and specifically named what she did well..."
  - **I:** "...I saw her confidence visibly increase and she volunteered to lead the next section. Your recognition made a tangible difference."
  - **Invitation:** "Keep doing this. It's exactly the kind of leadership that builds our team."

<!-- notes: Most leaders dramatically underuse positive SBI feedback. Reinforcing feedback is not "nice" -- it is strategic. It tells people exactly what to keep doing and why. The ratio recommendation from Marcial Losada's research: at least 3:1 positive to corrective for high-performing teams. Ask participants to think of one reinforcing and one corrective SBI they need to give this week. -->

## Exercise: Feedback Delivery Practice

- **Pair up** (same partner as SBI writing)
- **Round 1 (3 minutes):** Deliver your **corrective** SBI feedback to your partner as if they were the real person. Partner gives feedback on the delivery:
  - Was it specific enough?
  - Did it feel like an attack or a conversation?
  - Would you be open to this feedback?
- **Round 2 (3 minutes):** Switch roles
- **Coaching tips for delivery:**
  - Start with your intent ("I'm sharing this because...")
  - Use a calm, matter-of-fact tone
  - Pause after the Impact -- give them space to respond
  - End with a question, not a statement

<!-- notes: This practice round is where the learning becomes embodied. Observing someone deliver feedback and giving them real-time coaching on tone, specificity, and invitation is far more powerful than reading about SBI. Common adjustments: tone too harsh, jumping to solutions before the other person speaks, using "you always" or "you never" instead of specific behaviors. -->

## Receiving Feedback: The Other Side

- Giving feedback is hard. **Receiving** feedback is harder
- Sheila Heen and Douglas Stone (*Thanks for the Feedback*) identify three triggers that block us from hearing feedback:
  1. **Truth triggers:** "That's not accurate" -- we reject the content
  2. **Relationship triggers:** "Who are YOU to tell me?" -- we reject the source
  3. **Identity triggers:** "Am I fundamentally flawed?" -- we feel threatened at the core
- **Strategies for receiving feedback well:**
  - Assume positive intent (even if delivery is poor)
  - Listen for the **2% that is true** -- even unfair feedback contains a kernel
  - Ask clarifying questions: "Can you give me an example?"
  - Thank the person -- giving feedback is a risk, and they took it for you
  - Separate deciding from reacting -- you can evaluate the feedback later

<!-- notes: This section is often a relief for participants who have been thinking about giving feedback. Receiving is equally important. The "2% rule" is powerful: even if feedback is 98% unfair, finding the 2% that is true makes you better. Ask: "Think of feedback you initially rejected but later realized was accurate. What shifted?" The identity trigger is the most powerful -- when feedback touches our sense of who we are, we either shut down or overreact. -->

## Building a Feedback Culture

- Individual feedback skill is necessary but insufficient -- you need a **culture** of feedback
- Elements of a feedback culture:
  - **Frequency:** Feedback is a daily practice, not an annual event
  - **Direction:** Feedback flows in all directions -- up, down, and across
  - **Norms:** The team has explicit agreements about how feedback is given and received
  - **Safety:** People trust that feedback is given with positive intent
  - **Modeling:** The leader asks for feedback FIRST and responds non-defensively
- **The leader's role:** Ask your team, "What is one thing I could do differently that would make you more effective?" Then actually change

> A culture of feedback is not built by training -- it is built by the daily behavior of the leader.

<!-- notes: The most powerful action a leader can take: ask for feedback publicly, receive it graciously, and visibly act on it. This single behavior does more to build a feedback culture than any training program. Challenge participants: "Will you ask your team for feedback this week? And when you receive it, will you thank them and act on it?" -->

## Integrating Safety, Coaching & Feedback

- These three capabilities form a **virtuous cycle:**
  - **Psychological safety** enables honest coaching conversations
  - **Coaching** develops people's capability and confidence
  - **Feedback** provides the specific data that accelerates growth
  - Growth reinforces safety -- people see that vulnerability leads to development, not punishment
- Without safety, coaching feels like interrogation and feedback feels like attack
- Without coaching, feedback has no development context
- Without feedback, coaching lacks the specific behavioral data that drives change

<!-- notes: This integration slide ties the three sections together. Draw the cycle on the flipchart: Safety -> Coaching -> Feedback -> Growth -> Safety. The key insight: you cannot just implement one of these in isolation. A leader who gives great feedback but hasn't created safety will do more harm than good. A leader who creates safety but never coaches wastes the potential of that environment. -->
