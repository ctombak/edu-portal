This module combines two essential capabilities: (1) accurately diagnosing whether you're facing a technical problem or an adaptive challenge, and (2) managing yourself as the foundation for leading others.

## Technical Problems vs. Adaptive Challenges — The Heifetz Framework

Ronald Heifetz at Harvard identifies two fundamentally different types of challenges. Technical problems are clear with known solutions and existing expertise. Adaptive challenges are unclear, require learning, and demand that people change beliefs, habits, or priorities. Examples in institutional settings: Low morale isn't solved by a team-building event when trust has eroded. Missed deadlines aren't fixed by better software when workload exceeds capacity. Change resistance isn't overcome by more communication when people's identities feel threatened.

> The single biggest failure of leadership is to treat adaptive challenges like technical problems.

**Framework: Technical vs. Adaptive** — Technical: clear, known solutions, existing expertise sufficient, can be implemented by authority. Adaptive: unclear, requires learning, people must change beliefs/habits/priorities, no expert has the answer.

## The Balcony and the Dance Floor

On the dance floor, you're engaged in action, responding to immediate demands, seeing what's right in front of you. On the balcony, you're observing the whole system, noticing invisible patterns, seeing how pieces connect, understanding the bigger picture. Great leaders develop the capacity to move between both. Diagnostic questions from the balcony: What's the real problem? Who's in the system? What are the competing commitments? What's the history? What's my role in this system? What would need to change?

> Great leaders develop the capacity to move between dance floor and balcony—staying engaged while maintaining perspective.

## Self-Awareness: Your Leadership Defaults

Leaders tend to have default orientations under pressure. The Analyst sees complexity, gathers data, thinks systemically—but can over-analyze and miss emotional dynamics. The Driver is decisive and action-oriented—but can move too fast and create resistance. The Harmonizer builds consensus and reads people—but can avoid conflict and delay difficult decisions. The Visionary sees possibilities and inspires—but can miss details and frustrate pragmatists.

> All four orientations are valuable. The question is whether you have the range to flex when the situation requires a different approach.

**Framework: Four Leadership Orientations** — Analyst (systematic, may over-analyze), Driver (decisive, may miss stakeholders), Harmonizer (consensus, may avoid conflict), Visionary (possibilities, may miss details). All valuable—the goal is developing range.

## Energy Management & The Five Practices of Adaptive Leadership

Leadership starts with self-regulation. If you can't manage your own triggers and energy, you can't manage a department. Based on Heifetz's work, adaptive leaders consistently practice five disciplines: (1) Get on the balcony—step back to see the system, (2) Identify the adaptive challenge—distinguish technical fixes from people change, (3) Regulate distress—keep people in productive discomfort, (4) Maintain disciplined attention—keep focus on difficult work, (5) Give the work back—resist providing all answers and develop others' capacity.

**Framework: Five Practices of Adaptive Leadership** — Get on the balcony, Identify the adaptive challenge, Regulate distress, Maintain disciplined attention, Give the work back.
