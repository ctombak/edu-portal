Accountability, ownership, and effective delegation are the foundation of effective leadership. This module addresses how leaders take full responsibility for outcomes, delegate to develop capability, make decisions in complexity, and drive change.

## Extreme Ownership & Accountability

From Jocko Willink & Leif Babin: Leaders must own everything in their world. There is no one else to blame. The Ownership Mindset: 'What could I have done differently?' vs. 'It wasn't my fault.' 'I'm responsible for the outcome' vs. 'I did my part.' 'How can I enable my team to succeed?' vs. 'My team let me down.' What it looks like: own the outcome not just the task, no excuses, own your team's failures, support other teams, and lead both up and down. Critical distinction: Accountability is forward-looking ('How do we fix this?'), blame is backward-looking ('Whose fault is this?').

> The more you own, the more you grow. The more you blame, the more you stay stuck.

**Framework: Extreme Ownership** — Own the outcome, no excuses, own team failures, cover and move, lead up and down. Accountability builds trust; blame erodes it.

## The Delegating Leader — Skill/Will Matrix

Leaders fail to delegate for predictable reasons: 'It's faster if I do it myself,' 'No one can do it as well as me,' 'I don't want to overload my team.' The cost: you become the bottleneck, your team doesn't grow, you burn out. Match delegation approach to the person: Direct (low skill/will—clear instructions, monitoring), Motivate (low skill, high will—teach, coach, encourage), Challenge (high skill, low will—investigate, stretch), Delegate (high skill, high will—autonomy, outcomes not methods). The delegation conversation: What (clear outcome), Why (purpose), Boundaries (authority), Support (resources), Check-in (follow-up, not micromanagement).

**Framework: Skill/Will Delegation Matrix** — Direct (low/low), Motivate (low skill/high will), Challenge (high skill/low will), Delegate (high/high). Match your approach to where the person is.

## Strategic Decision-Making — The RAPID Framework

One common failure: unclear decision rights. RAPID clarifies roles: Recommend (gather data, analyze, propose), Agree (must agree for decision to proceed), Perform (implement the decision), Input (consulted, provide perspective), Decide (makes the final call). Key biases to watch: Confirmation Bias (seek disconfirming evidence), Sunk Cost Fallacy (evaluate future value only), Groupthink (assign devil's advocate), Authority Bias (create safe challenge culture).

**Framework: RAPID Decision Framework** — Recommend, Agree, Perform, Input, Decide. Clarifies who does what in complex decisions.

## Leading Change in Institutions

The Change Equation: D × V × F > R. Dissatisfaction with status quo × Vision of a better future × First achievable steps must exceed Resistance. If any factor is zero, change fails. Understanding resistance: it's not opposition—it's information. Sources include loss of control ('This is happening TO me'), uncertainty ('What does this mean for me?'), competence concerns ('I'm not sure I can do this'), and past failures ('We've been burned before'). Coalition building: start with the 'movable middle,' find champions with credibility, create early wins, address skeptics directly.

> Resistance isn't opposition—it's information.

**Framework: Change Equation** — D × V × F > R. Dissatisfaction × Vision × First steps must exceed Resistance. All three positive factors must be present.
