Leadership happens in conversations. The quality of your conversations—coaching, feedback, navigating conflict—determines your effectiveness.

## Psychological Safety & the Fail-Fast Culture

Amy Edmondson's research: teams that report more mistakes are actually higher-performing—because they're learning, not hiding. Google's Project Aristotle found psychological safety was the #1 predictor of high-performing teams. Fear kills innovation, drives information underground, and creates compliance rather than commitment. The Fail-Fast, Learn-Faster mindset means running small experiments quickly, learning from failures openly, iterating based on real feedback, treating failures as data, and stopping bad ideas early. Leaders enable this by modeling their own failures, celebrating learning not just success, creating safe zones, and separating 'intelligent failures' from 'preventable failures.'

> People don't follow leaders they fear—they simply comply until they find an exit.

**Framework: Psychological Safety (Edmondson)** — #1 predictor of team performance. Teams that report more mistakes perform better because they're learning, not hiding.

**Framework: Fail-Fast Culture Elements** — Run small experiments, learn from failures openly, iterate on feedback, treat failures as data, stop bad ideas early.

## The Coaching Habit — The GROW Model

In the old model, leaders had answers. In the new model, leaders who ask good questions outperform those who give good answers. The GROW Model: Goal (What do you want to achieve?), Reality (What's happening now? What have you tried?), Options (What could you do? What else?), Will (What will you do? When? How can I support you?). Coaching develops capability rather than dependency—questions activate ownership while answers create passivity.

> Leaders who ask good questions outperform those who give good answers.

**Framework: GROW Coaching Model** — Goal → Reality → Options → Will. A structured approach to coaching that develops capability through questions rather than answers.

## Feedback That Works — The SBI Framework

Most people want more feedback yet dread receiving it and avoid giving it. Poorly delivered feedback feels like attack and triggers identity concerns. The SBI Framework works for both corrective AND reinforcing feedback: Situation (specific time and place—grounds in reality), Behavior (observable actions—focuses on what they DID, not who they ARE), Impact (effect on you, team, or work—explains why it matters). Adding a fourth element: share your intent and invite their perspective.

**Framework: SBI Feedback** — Situation (when/where) → Behavior (what they did, observable) → Impact (the effect). Works for both positive reinforcement and corrective feedback.
